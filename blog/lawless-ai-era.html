Our Lawless AI Era: When Technology Outpaces the Rules

Published 14 February 2026 · By Joziane El Hawi · 8 min read

When the motor car arrived, it arrived faster than the rules for it. Licensing, driver training, traffic signals, lane markings and speed limits were not designed in advance. They were assembled, piece by piece, over years, in response to harm that had already occurred. For a period, a powerful and genuinely useful technology was widely available and only loosely governed.

Today's rollout of artificial intelligence rhymes with that period. Powerful tools are broadly accessible. The norms, the training and the rules are following behind. The gap between capability and governance is where avoidable mistakes happen.

The rules are arriving, unevenly

It would be wrong to say AI is unregulated. The more accurate description is that regulation is arriving in an uneven patchwork, at different speeds in different places.

The European Union's AI Act entered into force in August 2024 and is being applied in stages. Its prohibitions on certain practices and its AI-literacy obligations began applying in February 2025; obligations for general-purpose AI models followed in August 2025; further high-risk provisions are staged into 2026 and beyond, with timelines that have themselves been subject to amendment. In the United States, in the absence of a comprehensive federal statute, individual states have moved: California, Colorado, New York and others have enacted AI laws covering automated decisions, training-data transparency and related obligations, with various effective dates around 2026. Other jurisdictions, including South Korea and China, have introduced their own binding frameworks.

For an organisation operating across borders, the result is not a single rulebook but a map that has to be read jurisdiction by jurisdiction.

The direction of travel is clear enough: more rules, more enforcement, more expectation that organisations can show how their AI systems work. But the patchwork means that "wait for the law to settle" is not a workable strategy. The law will not present itself as a single finished document on a known date.

The driving analogy, taken seriously

This is the comparison at the centre of how we think about AI. You would not hand someone the keys to a car and simply wish them luck. Driving is a capability that requires rules, training and practised habits, and we treat it that way as a matter of course.

AI deserves the same seriousness. The technology itself is not the problem. The problem is the absence of an operating standard around it: the shared understanding of what good use looks like, what is out of bounds, what must be checked and who is accountable. That standard is something an organisation builds. It does not arrive on its own.

What a team can do now

The encouraging part of the analogy is that the wait need not be repeated. With the car, it took years and real harm before society agreed that driver training was essential. An organisation adopting AI today does not have to relearn that lesson slowly.

It can decide now to give its people the equivalent of driver training: literacy in what these tools do and where they fail, clear internal rules, verification habits and a defined sense of accountability. Doing so does not require waiting for any particular law to be finalised, because none of it conflicts with where regulation is heading; it anticipates it.

The era is loosely governed, for now. That is a reason to build your own standard, not a reason to operate without one. The organisations that will look prepared in a few years are the ones writing that standard today.
