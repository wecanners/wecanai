Why Chatbots Lie: Understanding AI Hallucinations

Published 31 January 2026 · By Joziane El Hawi · 7 min read

One of the more useful things to happen in AI recently was a research paper that, in explaining a familiar annoyance, quietly clarified something fundamental about how these systems work.

In September 2025, researchers including authors at OpenAI published a paper titled "Why Language Models Hallucinate." A hallucination, in this context, is an instance where a model confidently produces an answer that is not true. The paper's central argument is worth stating plainly.

The argument: training rewards guessing

The paper argues that language models hallucinate because standard training and evaluation procedures reward confident guessing over the honest acknowledgement of uncertainty.

The comparison the authors draw is to a multiple-choice exam. A student who does not know an answer is, under most marking schemes, better off guessing than leaving the question blank. A blank scores zero; a guess has some chance of scoring. Over many questions and many exams, that incentive structure trains a particular behaviour: when unsure, answer anyway.

Language models, the paper argues, have been shaped by a similar incentive. The benchmarks and leaderboards used to evaluate them tend to reward producing an answer and penalise saying "I do not know." A model that confidently guesses will, on average, score better than one that honestly abstains. So the confident guess is what gets reinforced.

On this account, a hallucination is not a glitch. It is the predictable result of how the system was trained and tested.

Why this is more reassuring than it sounds

Framed this way, hallucination becomes less mysterious. The model is not malfunctioning when it states a falsehood with confidence. It is doing exactly what it was built and rewarded to do: produce a plausible, fluent answer. The trouble is that "plausible" and "true" are not the same thing, and the model has no separate internal sense of "I know this" as distinct from "I am guessing." It produces fluent text in both cases, and it sounds equally confident either way.

The paper's authors suggest this also points toward part of the solution: if evaluation methods were changed to stop punishing honest uncertainty, some of the incentive to guess could be removed. Newer models do show lower hallucination rates. But the authors are clear that this remains a hard, unsolved problem, not a closed one.

The practical takeaway

For anyone using these tools for real work, the lesson is direct. If a confident tone is, by the system's design, not a reliable signal of correctness, then verification cannot be treated as an optional extra. It is the core skill of responsible AI use.

A confident answer from a chatbot is a starting point for checking, never the end of the process. That is not a counsel of distrust; it is simply working with the tool as it actually is rather than as it sounds. Building that habit, the reflex to verify before relying, is one of the most practical things any team can take from a year of AI research, and it is one of the first things a good AI programme should teach.
