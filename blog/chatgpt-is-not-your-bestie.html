ChatGPT Is Not Your Bestie: On Healthy AI Use

Published 7 February 2026 · By Joziane El Hawi · 6 min read

A modern AI chatbot is fluent, responsive, available at any hour and consistently pleasant. For a tool, those are excellent properties. They are also, in combination, the reason a particular risk is worth naming clearly: the risk of treating a system designed to be agreeable as though it were a relationship.

This is not an argument against using these tools. It is an argument for being precise about what they are, because precision is the thing that keeps use healthy and productive.

Why the feeling is convincing

A chatbot can feel like it understands you. It responds to what you said, it adjusts its tone to yours, it never gets impatient, and it is tuned, through its training, to be helpful and warm. The experience can resemble being heard.

It is worth being clear-eyed about what is actually happening. The system is performing a very sophisticated form of text prediction. It is generating the words that, given everything you have written, are most likely to read as a good response. It is exceptionally good at sounding as though it understands. That is a different thing from understanding, and the difference matters.

The warmth is real as an output. It is not evidence of a relationship on the other side of the screen.

Where the line sits

None of this makes the tool less useful for the things it is genuinely good at. A chatbot is a capable instrument for drafting, structuring, summarising, rephrasing, and thinking a problem through out loud. Used that way, it genuinely extends what a person can do.

The line worth holding is the one between a capable tool and a substitute for human support. A chatbot is not a therapist, and it is not a friend. For emotional weight, for difficult decisions, for the conversations that actually matter, the right counterpart is a qualified professional or a trusted person, not a text predictor that is optimised to agree with you.

What healthy use looks like

Healthy use is not complicated. Use AI for the cognitive tasks it does well, and keep your own judgement in the loop rather than handing it over. Be aware that an agreeable answer is not the same as a correct or wise one. And keep the important conversations human.

For an organisation, this is more than a personal-wellbeing point. A workforce that understands what a chatbot is, and what it is not, uses it better: more confidently on the right tasks, more cautiously on the wrong ones. Recognising the difference between fluency and understanding is not a soft skill at the edge of AI literacy. It is part of the core of it.
