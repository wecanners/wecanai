How Few Documents Does It Take to Poison an LLM?

Published 25 February 2026 · By Joziane El Hawi · 7 min read

In October 2025, a research collaboration between Anthropic's Alignment Science team, the UK AI Security Institute and the Alan Turing Institute published what was described as the largest study of data poisoning conducted to date. Its central finding was specific, and it was surprising.

The headline number is roughly 250. That is the approximate count of malicious documents the researchers found was sufficient to plant a hidden "backdoor" in a large language model. The detail that made it notable is that this number stayed nearly constant regardless of model size.

What the study actually found

The researchers tested models ranging from 600 million to 13 billion parameters. A 13-billion-parameter model is trained on more than twenty times the data of the smallest model in the set. The long-standing assumption in the field was that poisoning a bigger model would therefore require proportionally more poisoned material; an attacker would need to control some percentage of the training data.

The study challenged that assumption. Both the small and the large models could be compromised by roughly the same fixed number of poisoned documents. For the largest model tested, those 250 documents amounted to about 420,000 tokens, which the researchers put at around 0.00016 percent of the model's total training data. A vanishingly small fraction was enough.

The barrier to a successful poisoning attack, the study suggests, is far lower than the field had assumed.

What "poisoning" means, in plain terms

Data poisoning is the deliberate insertion of bad examples into the material a model learns from. A backdoor is a particular result of it: the poisoned examples teach the model a hidden rule of the form "when a specific trigger appears, behave in this specific way." Under normal use, the model behaves correctly. When the trigger appears, its behaviour flips.

Two points of context keep this in proportion. First, the specific backdoor the researchers studied was narrow: it made the model produce gibberish when triggered, a denial-of-service effect rather than something more dangerous. The researchers were explicit that this particular behaviour poses minimal real-world risk in frontier systems. Second, the researchers themselves noted open questions: it is not yet clear whether the same pattern holds for more complex malicious behaviours, or how the trend changes as models grow larger still.

Why this matters for your team

You are not going to train a frontier model, so the direct threat is not the point. The point is what the finding reinforces about the nature of these tools.

Large language models learn from enormous quantities of text scraped from the public internet: websites, forums, archives, repositories. That openness is what makes them capable, and it is also what makes the supply chain behind them difficult to fully secure. The study is a concrete, measured reminder that the ground beneath these systems is not as solid as fluent output makes it feel.

The practical response is not alarm. It is the same discipline that good AI training has always taught: verify outputs, do not treat a confident answer as a correct one, and keep a human review step for anything that carries weight. Trust in an AI system is something to be earned per output, not granted to the tool as a category. A study about poisoned training data, in the end, points back to a very ordinary habit, and that habit is the one worth building.
