How Many Documents Does It Take to Break an LLM?

Published 25 February 2026 · By Joziane El Hawi

The question sounds dramatic, and the answer is dramatic too, because it changes how everyday users should think about AI risk.

A research write-up led by Anthropic, with the UK AI Security Institute and the Alan Turing Institute, reported that a small number of malicious documents was enough to plant a hidden trigger-and-response behavior in a model. The unsettling part is not only the number. It is that the number was much smaller than people had assumed for large models.

What "poisoning" means in plain terms

Data poisoning is when bad examples slipped into training data teach a model a hidden rule: if a particular trigger appears, behave a certain way. Because modern models learn from enormous text collections, a small set of carefully crafted documents can have an outsized effect.

Why this matters for your team

You will not be training a frontier model. But the finding reinforces a habit we teach in every program: verify outputs, do not assume a confident answer is a correct one, and keep a human review step for anything that matters. Trust is earned per output, not granted to the tool.
