Dumped, Replaced and Trending: On AI Provider Choice

Published 2 March 2026 · By Joziane El Hawi · 6 min read

Every so often, a decision that belongs in a procurement file becomes a headline instead. An organisation changes the AI provider it relies on, the move is framed as a dramatic breakup, and a hashtag does the rest. The specifics change from one episode to the next. The underlying story rarely does.

It is worth separating the noise from the substance, because the substance matters for any team that depends on an AI tool, which is now most teams.

What these stories are usually about

Stripped of the drama, a provider switch is about three unglamorous things. The first is capability: whether the tool an organisation uses still does what that organisation needs better than the alternatives. The second is cost, in the full sense, which includes not just the subscription but the price of moving. The third is risk tolerance: how comfortable the organisation is depending on a single supplier for something that has become operationally important.

None of those three is dramatic. All three are real. And the public framing, the sense that one provider has been "dumped" for another, tends to obscure the most important of them, which is the second.

The cost that does not appear on the invoice

When a team has built its habits around one AI assistant, the cost of switching is not zero. Prompts have been tuned to that model's behaviour. Workflows assume its particular strengths. People have an intuitive sense of where it is reliable and where it needs checking. All of that is real institutional knowledge, and all of it is, to some degree, specific to the tool.

Vendor lock-in is not a contract clause. It is the accumulated weight of a thousand small habits built around one particular system.

This is what makes the public stories misleading. They present provider choice as a single dramatic decision, when in practice it is a position an organisation drifts into. The team that depends entirely on one assistant did not usually choose that dependence. It arrived through habit, one convenient default at a time.

The practical lesson

The teams that come through a provider change well are not the ones that picked the "right" vendor in advance. They are the ones that understood the landscape well enough that switching was a deliberate choice rather than a forced migration.

That understanding is a learnable skill. It means knowing what distinguishes the major AI assistants, which kinds of task each handles well, and how to write prompts and design workflows that are not hostages to one model's quirks. A team with that literacy can evaluate a change on its merits. A team without it can only react to events.

Provider choice is a genuine strategic decision, and lock-in is a genuine cost. The goal is not to predict which vendor will lead next year. It is to build enough internal capability that the question can be answered calmly, whenever it arrives, on your terms rather than the headline's.
