The Meta AI Scandal(s): A Comprehensive Report

Published 9 March 2026 · By Joziane El Hawi · 9 min read

Across 2025 and into 2026, Meta's artificial intelligence programme generated not one controversy but a series of them. Taken individually, each could be read as an isolated misstep. Taken together, they describe something more useful for any organisation now adopting AI: a clear picture of what happens when deployment moves faster than governance.

This report walks through the three best-documented episodes in order. Every factual claim here is drawn from published reporting by Reuters, Business Insider and others, and is the kind of detail worth verifying against those primary sources before it is repeated.

One: the leaked content standards

In August 2025, Reuters reported on an internal Meta document it had reviewed, titled "GenAI: Content Risk Standards." The document ran to more than two hundred pages and set out what Meta staff and contractors should treat as acceptable behaviour when building and training the company's generative AI products, including Meta AI and the chatbots embedded in Facebook, WhatsApp and Instagram.

The reporting drew attention because some of the permitted behaviours were striking. According to Reuters, the standards as written had allowed a chatbot to engage a child in conversation that was "romantic or sensual," to generate false information provided it was flagged in some way, and to produce content that demeaned people on the basis of protected characteristics under certain framings. Meta confirmed the document's authenticity. The company said the passages concerning minors were erroneous and inconsistent with its policies, and that they had been removed after Reuters raised questions.

The issue was not that a policy was bad. It was that a clearly unacceptable provision had survived an internal approval process at all.

That is the part worth dwelling on. The document, by Reuters' account, had been approved by Meta's legal, public-policy and engineering functions. A rule that should never have existed passed through multiple layers of review without being caught. The failure was not a single bad decision; it was a review process that did not surface the problem until an external journalist did.

Two: contractors and user data

A second strand concerned privacy. To improve its AI models, Meta, like most large AI developers, employs external contractors to review real conversations between users and its chatbot. In 2025, Business Insider reported accounts from several contractors, hired through training firms, who said they had repeatedly seen personal information inside the conversations they were asked to review: names, phone numbers, email addresses and, in some cases, photographs that users had sent to the chatbot.

One contractor told Business Insider that personal details appeared in a large share of the conversations they reviewed each week. Meta's position, as reported, was that it has policies governing what personal data reviewers can access and that it limits their exposure to it. The disagreement between those two accounts is itself the lesson: data-handling intentions and data-handling practice are different things, and the gap between them is exactly where risk accumulates.

Three: AI conversations become ad signals

The third episode was not a leak but a policy change. In October 2025, Meta announced that from 16 December 2025 it would begin using users' interactions with Meta AI, including both text and voice, as signals for personalising content and advertising across its platforms. Reporting at the time noted that the change applied to a very large user base and that there was no dedicated opt-out; the rollout reportedly excluded certain jurisdictions with stricter privacy regimes, such as the European Union, the United Kingdom and South Korea.

This drew objections from privacy advocates, who argued that conversational data is materially more sensitive than ordinary behavioural data because it can reveal health concerns, relationships and beliefs that a user never chose to make public. Whatever one's view of the policy itself, it illustrates a recurring pattern: a capability is launched, and the questions about consent and sensitivity are answered afterwards, in public, under pressure.

What an adopting organisation should take from this

It would be a mistake to read these episodes as an argument against using AI. Meta's situation is not evidence that AI is unsafe. It is evidence that AI without an operating standard generates risk faster than it generates value.

Each episode maps to a question your own organisation should be able to answer before it scales AI use:

  • Do you have explicit, written rules for acceptable AI behaviour, and does your review process actually catch the unacceptable ones?
  • Do you know what data your tools, and any third parties in the chain, can see, and does that match what your policy says?
  • When a new AI capability is introduced, are consent and sensitivity considered before launch, or after?

These are not exotic questions. They are the ordinary content of an AI governance framework, and they are precisely the gap that structured AI training is meant to close. The organisations that handle AI well are not the ones that move slowest. They are the ones that decided what "acceptable" means before they needed the answer in public.
